Algebraic Structures

The following algebraic structures together with their corresponding homomorphisms form categories. Also all algebraic structures share the following terminology:

Let (E,⋅) have any algebraic structure T.
sub-T
U≤E :⇔ U⊆E ∧ (U,⋅|U×U) has itself an algebraic structure T.
U is called a sub-T then.
isomorphism
Let E,F have the algebraic structure T, and φ:E→F be a map.
If Then φ is a
(iso) ∃ψ:F→E homomorphism with φ∘ψ=idF ∧ ψ∘φ=idE isomorphism of T ⊂ homomorphism of T

This condition for isomorphisms will often be equivalent to φ being a bijective homomorphism.

Note:
Just like the definition above, several of the following definitions for algebraic structures and homomorphisms are meant cumulative. This is written symbolically as f.ex. "group ⊂ monoid" which means that a set is a group if it is a monoid and satisfies the additional properties of groups (which would be (i) in this particular case). In turn, a set is a monoid if it is a semigroup and satisfies (n), etc. This notation saves a lot of space and is convenient for usual cases.

Groups

Algebraic structures with one law of composition
Let M be a set.
If Then M is a
(s) ⋅:M×M→M is a law of composition "stable" magma
(a) ∀x,y,z∈M (x⋅y)⋅z = x⋅(y⋅z) "associative" semigroup ⊂ magma
(n) ∃1∈M ∀x∈M 1⋅x = x = x⋅1 "neutral" monoid ⊂ semigroup
(i) ∀x∈M ∃x-1∈M x⋅x-1 = 1 = x-1⋅x "inverses" group ⊂ monoid
(c) ∀x,y∈M x⋅y = y⋅x "commutative" Abelian group ⊂ group
Note:

There are names for other structures as well, like unital magma for a magma satisfying (n), but they are less common. In case of multiplication the sign ⋅ is often omitted. It is always true that a set can have at most one neutral element, and at most one inverse of any element.  M× := {x∈M ¦ x invertible} is called the unit group of the monoid M.

Homomorphisms of algebraic structures with one law of composition
Let (M,⋅), (N,∗) be magmas/semigroups/monoids/groups/Abelian groups, and φ:M→N a map.
If Then φ is a
(m) ∀x,y∈M φ(x⋅y) = φ(x)∗φ(y) "morph" homomorphism of ...
(1) φ(1) = 1 "unital" homomorphism of monoids ⊂
homomorphism of semigroups
Note:

Remember that the definitions for homomorphisms are meant cumulative. This means that for φ to be a homomorphism of monoids, both (m) and (1) must be true. In fact, although both of these must also be true for homomorphisms of groups, the property (1) already follows from (m) provided that M, N are groups. So the property (1) only must be verified for monoids, which is the justification for the suggestive notation in the table above.

If φ:M→N is a homomorphism (of magmas), it carries much of the algebraic structure of M over to f(M)⊆N. (f(M),∗) inherits the properties (s),(a),(n),(i),(c) from (M,⋅), however in general it may be f(1)≠1.

  1. For every set X there is the free magma M(X) := .
  2. For every set X there is the free semigroup (of words) Fa(X) := X+ := ⋃n∈N\{0} Xn.
  3. For every set X there is the free monioid (of words) Mo(X) := X* := ⋃n∈N Xn.
  4. For every set X there is the free group F(X) with basis X with
    ∀y∈F(X) ∃!x1ε1⋅...⋅xnεn = y with xi∈X ∧ εi∈{-1,1} ∧ xiεi≠xi+1-εi+1.

Rings

Algebraic structures with two laws of composition
Let R be a set.
If Then R is a
( )
  • (R,+) is an Abelian group
  • (R,⋅) is a semigroup
  • (d) ⋅ is distributive over +, i.e.
    • ∀x,y,z∈R x⋅(y+z) = (x⋅y) + (x⋅z)
    • ∀x,y,z∈R (x+y)⋅z = (x⋅z) + (y⋅z)
 pseudo-ring
( ) (R,⋅) is a monoid ring with 1 ⊂ pseudo-ring
( ) (R,⋅) is commutative commutative ring with 1 ⊂ ring with 1
( ) (R\{0},⋅) is a group, resp. R× = R\{0} skew field ⊂ ring with 1
( ) (R\{0},⋅) commutative group. field
= commutative ring ∩ skew field
Note:

For a ring R, the neutral element in (R,+) is written as 0, the neutral element in (R,⋅) as 1.

☡Rings with 1 are simply called rings, here. However, be careful about the distinction of pseudo-rings and true rings with 1. Some authors also refer to a ring when they think of what we called a pseudo-ring, and will always speak of a ring with 1 if they mean true rings with 1.

A pseudo-ring R has the following simple properties

  • ∀x∈R x⋅0 = 0 = 0⋅x
  • ∀x,y∈R x⋅(-y) = -(x⋅y) = (-x)⋅y
  • ∀x,y∈R (-x)⋅(-y) = x⋅y
  • R≠{0} ⇔ 1≠0

If Then φ is a
(m)
  • φ:(R,+)→(R',+) is a homomorphism of groups
  • φ:(R,⋅)→(R',⋅) is a homomorphism of semigroups
 "morph" homomorphism of pseudo-rings
(1) φ(1) = 1 "unital" homomorphism of ... ⊂
homomorphism of pseudo-ring
Note:

Compare with homomorphisms of groups to see that for homomorphisms of skew-fields the property (1) follows from (m). Also note that homomorphisms of rings from a skew-field are injective, since Ring1→Grp; (R,+,⋅)↦R× is a covariant functor implying that homomorphisms φ satisfy φ(R×)⊆(R')×.

Modules

Algebraic structures with a law of composition and a law of action
Let R be a ring, M a set.
If Then M is a
( )
  • (M,+) is an Abelian group
  • ∗:R×M→M is a law of action with operators R.
  • (d) ∗ is distributive over +, i.e.
    • ∀α∈R ∀x,y∈M α∗(x+y) = (α∗x) + (α∗y)
    • ∴ modules are commutative groups with operators.
  • („d‟) ∀α,β∈R ∀x∈M (α+β)∗x = (α∗x) + (β∗x)
  • („a‟) ∀α,β∈A ∀x∈M α∗(β∗x) = (α⋅β)∗x (for left R-modules)
  • („n‟) ∀x∈M 1∗x = x
 left R-module
( ) The same as for left R-modules, except that
  • („a‟) ∀α,β∈A ∀x∈M α∗(β∗x) = (β⋅α)∗x (for right R-modules)

Therefore, the law of action may be suggestively written as ∗:M×R→M instead.

( ) R is a field left R-vector space ⊂
left R-module
( ) R is a field right R-vector space ⊂
right R-module

By an abuse of language, left R-modules are simply called R-modules, and left R-vector spaces are simply called R-vector spaces.

Homomorphisms of algebraic structures with a law of composition and a law of action
Let (M,+,∗), (M',+,∗) be R-modules/R-vector spaces, and φ:M→M' a map.
If Then φ is a
(m)
  • φ:(R,+)→(R',+) is a homomorphism of groups
  • ∀α∈R∀x∈M φ(α∗x) = α∗φ(x)
 "R-linear" homomorphism of ...

Examples

  1. A ring R is an R-module with ordinary ring multiplication, a (two-sided) ideal I⊴R is a (left and right) sub-R-module thereof.
    Be aware that proper ideals I⊲R (i.e. I⊴R ∧ I≠R) are only sub-pseudo-rings and no unital sub-rings since 1∉I.
  2. Ab≅Z-Mod.
  3. Ring≅Z-Algas.
  4. If M and M' are R-modules, HomR(M,M') := {φ:M→M' ¦ φ is R-linear} is an R-module with pointwise addition and scalar multiplication.
  5. M⌄ := M* := HomR(M,R) is called the dual module to M. If M is free with base B, then M* is free with the dual base B*:={b*:B→R; b'↦δbb' ¦ b∈B}.
  6. R(X) := ⊕x∈X R is the free R-module with basis X (X is a set).
    ⇔ X is R-linear independent and generates the module (i.e. =〈X〉).

If Then E is a
( )

Quotient Structures

Provided one of the following (equivalent) cases is satisfied

  1. Let φ:M→M be a homomorphism on M and x~y :⇔ φ(x)=φ(y) be the equivalence relation φ induces on M.
  2. Let f:M→M be an arbitrary mapping that is compatible with the equivalence relation ~ it induces on M, i.e. ....
  3. Let ~ ⊆ M×M be a congruence relation on M, i.e. an equivalence relation that satisfies
    ∀x1,…,xn,y1,…,yn∈M ((∀i xi~yi) ⇒ ⋆(x1,…,xn) ~ ⋆(y1,…,yn))
    for all operators ⋆∈Σ of any arity n, occuring in the Σ-algebra M.
Then the quotient
M/~ := M̄ := {x̄ ¦ x∈M} with
x̄ := -1f({f(x)}) = {y∈M ¦ x~y} for x∈M
has the same algebraic structure as M. If M is a magma, semi-group, monoid, group, Abelian group, ring, R-module, or vector space, then M/~ is as well. The laws of composition ⋆ and laws of action ∗ on M/~ are obtained by passing to the quotient:
x̄⋆ȳ = x⋆y
α∗x̄ = α∗x

It is true that M/~ is a partition of M

∀x̄,ȳ∈M (x̄∩ȳ=∅ xor x̄=ȳ)
Although conversely, every partition of M defines an equivalence relation, that relation does not need to be a congruence compatible with the algebraic structure of M.

Note that these congruence relations ~ on M often correspond bijectively to special substructures of M, and to (left or right) cosets .... . Examples are normal subgroups in groups, subgroups in Abelian groups, ideals in rings (i.e. sub-R-modules), submodules in R-modules. For example, if G is a group with a normal subgroup N⊴G, then there is a homomorphism φ with N=Ker(φ) inducing an equivalence relation ~, and

G/N := G/~ = {g⋅N:={g⋅n ¦ n∈N} ¦ g∈G} = {N⋅g:={n⋅g ¦ n∈N} ¦ g∈G}
is the set of left cosets or the set of right cosets. Although the left and right cosets are also defined for ordinary subgroups H≤G instead of N, they do not coincide with any equivalence classes of a quotient group in the general case. In either case, all cosets have the same cardinality and
[G:H] := number of left cosets = number of right cosets (= |G/H| if H⊴G).
is called the index of the subgroup H in G. It satisfies
|G| = |H|*[G:H]

Generating Systems

Let E have the algebraic structure T, and X⊆E be a subset.
generated
〈X〉 := ⋂X⊆S≤E S ≤ E
is the smallest stable sub-T of E containing X. It is called the sub-T generated by X.
  • For an R-module E=M, an alternative characterization by linear combinations of X⊆E is
    〈X〉 = R∗X := 〈{r∗x ¦ r∈R ∧ x∈X}〉 = {∑x∈X ax∗x ¦ ∀x∈X ax∈R ∧ ax=0 p.t. x∈X}
  • For ideals I⊴R (which are defined as sub-R-modules of R), the usual notation is (X) instead of 〈X〉, and we have the same alternative characterizations
    (X) := ⋂X⊆S⊴E S = X⋅R = {∑x∈X ax⋅x ¦ ∀x∈X ax∈R ∧ ax=0 p.t. x∈X}
  • For field extensions L/K, and a subset B⊆L we have an alternative characterization
    K[B] := 〈K∪B〉 = {f(a1,...,an) / g(a1,...,an) ¦ n≥1 ∧ f,g∈K[X1,...,Xn] ∧ ∀i ai∈B ∧ g(a1,...,an)≠0}
